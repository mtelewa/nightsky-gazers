Refractors

Also known as dioptric or lens telescopes, utilize lenses to gather and focus light. These telescopes feature a long, slender tube housing an objective lens at the front end and an eyepiece at the rear.
As light passes through the objective lens, it is refracted and converged to form an image at the focal point. Refractors are renowned for their crisp, high-contrast images, making them ideal for observing celestial objects such as the Moon, planets, and double stars. Additionally, their sealed tube design minimizes maintenance requirements, making them a popular choice among amateur astronomers.

Reflectors

Reflecting telescopes, a fundamental innovation in astronomy, revolutionized the way we observe and understand the cosmos. Unlike their refracting counterparts, reflecting telescopes utilize mirrors rather than lenses to gather and focus light, offering several advantages in terms of performance and versatility.
Reflecting telescopes have several advantages over refracting telescopes. For one, they eliminate chromatic aberration, a common issue in refractors caused by the dispersion of light into its component colors. This means that reflecting telescopes can produce sharper and clearer images without the distortion caused by chromatic aberration.

Newtonian

Newtonian telescopes excel in capturing faint, distant objects such as galaxies, nebulae, and star clusters due to their large aperture and excellent light-gathering capabilities. However, they may require occasional collimation, the alignment of their optical components, to maintain optimal performance.
Newtonian telescopes remain a favorite among amateur astronomers for their versatility, affordability, and ability to provide breathtaking views of the cosmos.
